Artivism: Coming Back and Reflecting on HB1080

By Perci Ereth, Director of Media and Marketing, SDYA

 

I remember when I was first pulled into activism - it was HB1057. A bill to do the same thing as HB1080. It was killed. I helped to do it. Now I feel guilty. The loss of the rights of trans youth sent me spiraling. I love South Dakota, but I also hate it. I’ll be okay, but I need to know my limits and abilities in fighting this fight. Everyone does. Not everyone is able to sustainably do this work, so it’s important that we not only have a few strong activists, but many advocates in various capacities - politics, the human services, the nonprofit sector. These signs were my own way of acting - artivism. I have always been an artist, but I have never found a time or a way to apply it. My ‘Harm not Help’ sign was the first I made - it’s not perfect, I don’t work with markers, but it speaks to the need to have political satire in this time. To show the hypocrisy of this act. ‘Harm not Help’ is a play on ‘Help not Harm’ - a string of campaigns to implement these laws in states across the country, pushed by the Family Heritage Alliance. The sign emulates the logo of said campaign.

 

 
 

The original logo has a scalpel drawing across the harm. But I didn’t want that connotation. So I added a noose. Because that's the harm - the loss of trans life. It was simple and has a connotation of suicide. The broken heart was logical - next to the help, it came across to me as hypocritical. Which HB1080 is. There was intention with my design. I also created the “Blood on the SD GOP’s Hands” sign, as well as the “Trans, Autistic, Proud”. There was talk of how ‘autistic kids are being manipulated into being trans’, ‘autistic kids are confused’ and the like. As an autistic person, this hurt more than the transphobia - it told me I was ‘too stupid’ to know who I am. In reality, it makes sense why autistic people are more likely to be trans - they socialize and learn gender differently from allistics (non-autistics). This is what intersectionality looks like - my identities are not exclusive from each other. And that’s okay. This was a middle finger to that very point that no one else would know unless they had listened to the committee hearings.

 

 
 
 
 

This rhetoric was exacerbated by Chloe Cole, a detranstioner who is autistic. Chloe is not from South Dakota, but has been touring the country and testifying in support of these types of bills. She is actually from California. Out of state testimony in support of these anti-trans bills has been prevalent in South Dakota since HB1057, and not helped by the allowance of virtual testimony - email, handouts, and call-ins. “The number of proponents from out of state testifying on these bills tells us that this is not a South Dakota issue - it isn’t happening here. If there was a detransitioner  in South Dakota, they would come forward, not hide like Chloe and other proponents claimed.” said Di Ereth, vice president of Black Hills Center for Equality and my amazing advocate of a mother.

Even though HB1080 doesn’t affect me directly as an adult, but it does open the door for bills to ban all gender affirming care at any age - something already happening in other states. It hurts to see others hurt. But we will prevail one day. Until then, I have to take care of myself, and my community in ways that work for me - not more bureaucratized organizations using my likeness, no more burning myself out because of guilt and compassion fatigue. I will be an activist, but I will also be an artist and learn to use my art for what I stand for.

 
 

About the Author:

 
 

Perci Ereth is a pagan non-binary artist and activist studying Human Services, Sociology, and American Indian Studies at Black Hills State University. Perci has worked with many nonprofits in South Dakota. Now, they are taking their role in this fight back into their own hands through art and SDYA.
You can see Perci’s *regular* art at @perciraynearts on Instagram.
